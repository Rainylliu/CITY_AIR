Commercial Productes

Smoke from commercial sources such as food vendor trucks and incense burning contributes to air pollution. Food trucks release pollutants such as fine particles and volatile organic compounds during cooking, while burning incense emits particulate matter (PM), volatile organic compounds (VOC), nitrogen dioxide (NO2) and carbon monoxide (CO).

Personal Action

Smoke from personal activities such as smoking and cooking in residential areas can contribute to localized air pollution in New York City. Smoking, especially tobacco smoke, emits harmful chemicals into the air, including fine particles and volatile organic compounds, which can reduce indoor air quality and, if poorly ventilated, may affect nearby outdoor air. In addition, cooking, especially using certain smoke-producing methods, may release particulate matter (PM), carbon monoxide (CO), and other pollutants into both indoor and outdoor environments.

Transportation Emissions

One of the most important contributors to air pollution in New York City is vehicular emissions. High population density and heavy traffic result in the release of large amounts of pollutants such as carbon monoxide (CO), nitrogen oxides (NOx), volatile organic compounds (VOCs), and particulate matter (PM). These emissions are a direct consequence of gasoline and diesel engines in vehicles .

Fuel oils & Natural gas

Residual heating oil, a heavy oil byproduct of crude oil refining, is linked to health issues. In New York City, it is a major source of various air pollutants, including fine particulate matter (PM2.5), sulfur dioxide (SO2), nitrogen oxides (NOx), and black carbon.
